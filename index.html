Abstract

[cite_start]Causal representation learning in the anti-causal setting—labels cause features rather than the reverse—presents unique challenges requiring specialized approaches[cite: 1]. [cite_start]We propose **Anti-Causal Invariant Abstractions (ACIA)**, a novel measure-theoretic framework for anti-causal representation learning[cite: 2]. [cite_start]ACIA employs a **two-level design**: low-level representations capture how labels generate observations, while high-level representations learn stable causal patterns across environment-specific variations[cite: 3]. [cite_start]ACIA addresses key limitations of existing approaches by: (1) accommodating perfect and imperfect interventions through **interventional kernels**, (2) eliminating dependency on explicit **causal structures**, (3) handling **high-dimensional data** effectively, and (4) providing **theoretical guarantees** for out-of-distribution generalization[cite: 4]. [cite_start]Experiments on synthetic and real-world medical datasets demonstrate that ACIA consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods in both accuracy and invariance metrics[cite: 5]. [cite_start]Furthermore, our theoretical results establish tight bounds on performance gaps between training and unseen environments, confirming the efficacy of our approach for robust anti-causal learning[cite: 6].

Key Contributions

The ACIA framework provides a robust solution for the anti-causal problem by achieving several critical advances:
    [cite_start]
  • It introduces a novel **measure-theoretic framework** designed specifically for the anti-causal setting[cite: 2].
    • [cite_start]
  • It uses a **two-level representation** (low-level for generative process, high-level for invariant patterns) to separate variable dependencies[cite: 3].
    • [cite_start]
  • It is flexible enough to handle both **perfect and imperfect interventions** using interventional kernels[cite: 4].
    • [cite_start]
  • It **does not require explicit causal structures** as input, simplifying its application to complex, high-dimensional data[cite: 4].
    • [cite_start]
  • It comes with **strong theoretical guarantees** for out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization, including tight performance bounds[cite: 4, 6].
